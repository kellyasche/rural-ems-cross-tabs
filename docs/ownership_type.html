Ownership crosstabs

Which type of ownership best describes your agency?

Respondents were provided the following options, listed in order of most respondents on top;

  • Public (60.8%, 113)
  • Private (39.2%, 73)

The metro response was eliminated from analysis.

The survey was broken into 7 different sections. The three sections in bold is what was included in the analysis due to the MN Department of Health providing incomplete data.   1. Characteristics questions

  7. Funding questions

The analysis used for the cross tabs is the typical chi-square test with p-value.

Addition to MN Department of Health Summary

The following narrative combines the executive summary from the MN Department of Health’s aggregate analysis of the survey with the relationships found in this analysis. Hopefully this will round out the results a bit better and point to more specific trends within the results.


Characteristics section

Key Findings

Most rural EMS agencies provide basic life support services to relatively small populations spread across large geographic areas. Along with low daily call volumes, these realities exacerbate the inability to create sustainable business and staffing models.

  • The median call volume is nearly one call per day
  • Over half of the agencies cover more than 200 square miles; 37 percent cover more than 300 square miles
  • Over half of the agencies serve populations of less than 5,000

However, it is worth noting that there were some differences in responses depending on an agencies type of ownership.

  • Owned, managed or receive support from a corporate health system: Although a small percentage of total responses (27%) said they were either owned, managed or receive support from a corporate healthy system, agencies that were privately owned or are a non-profit confirmed this relationship at a significantly higher than publicly owned agencies (58% vs. 9%).
  • Types of services provided: although a very large percentage of agencies responded that they provide basic life support (88.2% of total responses), publicly owned agencies provided this service at a significantly higher rate than privately owned or non-profit agencies (97% vs. 78%). On the flip side, privately owned or non-profit agencies responded that they provide advanced life support at a higher rate than publicly owned agencies (51% vs. 12%).
  • Staff compensation: although a majority of agencies responded that they pay their staff using a volunteer / on call compensation structure (57% of total responses), publicly owned agencies were much more likely to pay this way than privately owned or non-profit agencies (67% vs. 39%). On the flip side, agencies that were privately owned or a non-profit confirmed that they pay their staff at a significantly higher rate than publicly owned agencies (42% vs. 6%).
  • Using EMRs to staff ambulance: Although a majority of agencies (59%) responded that they do NOT use EMRs to staff their ambulance(s), privately owned or non-profit agencies responded this way at a significantly higher rate than publicly owned agencies (78% of responses vs. 46%).
  • Compensation comparison to other public safety agencies: Although a very small percentage (13%) of agencies said they paid their staff better compared to other public safety agencies, privately owned or non-profit agencies responded this way at a significantly higher rate than publicly owned agencies (22% vs. 8%).


Workforce sustainability section

Key Findings

Staffing shortages are a major challenge for rural EMS agencies. Eighty (80) percent utilize some version of volunteers to staff their operations. Roster sizes are decreasing, many staff are trained for multiple public functions and many call schedules are not covered 24 hours prior to the shifts.

  • The active staff roster decreased for half of the agencies from the previous year
  • Weekday daytime hours are the most difficult shifts to cover. However, all weekend and holiday shifts are also difficult to cover
  • About 60 percent of agencies have inadequate staff to cover their call schedule without undue burden to the agency.
  • Fifty-nine (59) percent of agencies do not have their shifts 100 percent covered at least 24 hours in advance

Recruitment and retention of ambulance personnel continues to be a significant challenge even though: * Emergency Medical Responders (EMRs) are now widely used to actively staff rural EMS agencies * Most agencies cover the full cost of the continuing education courses for their staff * Most eligible recipients take advantage of state EMS training reimbursement

However, it is worth noting that there were some differences in responses depending on an agencies type of ownership.

  • Difficult to recruit new staff: Although a majority of agencies (63%) said that staff recruitment was difficult, publicly owned agencies responded this way at a significantly higher rate than privately owned or non-profit agencies (70% vs. 51%).
  • Cross-training of EMS staff: Although a large majority of agencies (74%) said that their EMS staff were cross-trained in other publicly safety positions, publicly owned agencies responded this way at a significantly higher rate than privately owned or non-profit agencies (85% vs. 60%).
  • Obstacles to recruiting - lack of trained candidates: Although half of all agencies (50%) responded that a lack of trained candidates was a barrier to staff recruitment, it was a more significant issue for privately owned or non-profit agencies (66% of these agencies vs. 41% of publicly owned agencies).
  • Obstacles to recruiting - time commitment is too great: Although a majority of all agencies (67%) responded that the time commitment being too great as a barrier to staff recruitment, it was a more wide-spread issue among publicly owned agencies (81% of these agencies vs. 47% of privately owned or non-profit agencies).
  • Obstacles to recruiting - child care responsibilities: Although only 38% of all agencies said that child care responsibilities were a barrier to staff recruitment, it was a more widespread issue for publicly owned agencies (48% of these agencies vs. 28% of publicly owned or non-profit agencies).
  • Agency have a retention strategy: Half of all agencies responded that they have a retention strategy, however privately owned or non-profit agencies had one at a much higher rate than publicly owned agencies (68% vs. 41% respectively).
  • Obstacles to retention - excessive time commitment: Although a large majority of all agencies said that the excessive time commitment or scheduling conflicts were a barrier to staff retention, publicly owned agencies confirmed this barrier at a significantly higher rate than privately owned or non-profit agencies (82% vs. 57%)
  • Obstacles to retention - limited advancement opportunities: Although only 23% of all agencies said that limited advancement opportunities were a barrier to staff retention, privately owned or non-profit agencies confirmed this barrier at a significantly higher rate than publicly owned agencies (42% vs. 12%).
  • Obstacles to retention - employer not allowing staff to leave: Although only 37% of all agencies said that employers not allowing staff to leave work for a call was a barrier to staff retention, publicly owned agencies confirmed this barrier at a significantly higher rate than privately owned or non-profit agencies (46% vs. 26%).


Leadership section

Key Findings

The largest problems that rural EMS agency managers face today, as in the past, are recruitment of staff and filling call schedules. These two management tasks seem to form a Maslow’s Hierarchy of basic management needs (see Appendix C, Question 24). It may be that efforts to address other management issues will be fruitless and/or unwelcomed until these basic needs are addressed.

  • Sixty-nine (69) percent of rural EMS managers report difficulty recruiting staff despite the fact that 57.5 percent have a recruitment plan
  • Over half of rural EMS managers report difficulty staffing their schedule

High leadership turnover was defined in this survey as an EMS agency having had three or more managers in the past five years. Using this definition, there is remarkably low turnover of rural EMS managers. A high majority of EMS managers have an active role in developing their budgets, but generally are not directly involved in billing.

  • Eighty-six (86) percent of services did not have high turnover of their managers
  • Eighty-one (81) percent of rural EMS managers have a role in developing their annual budgets
  • Eighty-one (81) percent use other resources to bill, with billing agencies as the most common at fifty-two (52) percent

However, it is worth noting that there was one difference in responses depending on the type of ownership.

  • Management received training: Although a majority of agencies (54%) responded that their management has received formal leadership or management training, privately owned or non-profit agencies responded this way at a significantly higher rate than publicly owned agencies (67% vs. 46%).

Summary:

The big take-away is that the type of ownership influenced nearly all of these questions in a very consistent way. Private/non-profit ownership typically meant that they were more likely to respond to the following questions at a higher rate; * that they receive corporate health support, * they offer advanced life support, * pay their staff and were compensated better or the same as other public safety positions, and, * didn’t use EMS to staff their ambulances.

The opposite was true for publicly owned agencies. This type of ownership meant that they responded in the following way at a significantly higher rate than expected; * did not receive corporate health support, * offered basic life support over advanced life support, * did not pay staff and were compensated worse than other public safety positions, and, * used EMS to staff their ambulances.
